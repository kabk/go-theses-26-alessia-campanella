So, I’m not mute.

That’s probably the first thing I should mention. Not because it’s a big deal or anything, just it matters here, you know? Like how you’d mention what floor you work on or whether you take the bus or drive. It’s just information. Being not mute means I can talk whenever I want. That’s basically it.

I work for The Archive. It sounds fancy but it’s really not. I’m in scheduling and forms processing, which is exactly as boring as it sounds. I move paperwork around. Forms go from Department A to Department B, I make sure they get there, I update some spreadsheets. That’s my whole job. I’ve been doing it for six years now. Anyway. Let me explain how things work here, because if you’re not from around here it probably sounds weird.

The Mutes can’t talk for twenty-nine days at a time. Well, they can physically, their mouths work fine, but they’re not allowed to. It’s a legal thing. They wear these grey wristbands so you can tell who they are, and they carry tablets if they need to communicate something urgent. Mostly they don’t. They’re quiet. You get used to it. After twenty-nine days, they can apply to speak. There’s this form, Form 7-C, and they submit it to us. Then someone in Review looks at it and decides if they can have Provisional Speech, which is like, one hour in a special room where they’re allowed to talk. If that goes well, they can apply for longer periods. If not, back to silence for another twenty-nine days. The whole time they’re in that monitoring period, those twenty-nine days, everything’s being recorded. Archive data, behavioral notes, all of it. They know they’re being watched. It’s standard procedure. Everything gets analyzed and filed before the Review decision comes down. Most of them get denied, though. I see the forms come through my desk every day. Lots of red “DENIED” stamps. I don’t know exactly what the criteria are, that’s above my pay grade, but whatever they’re looking for, most people don’t have it.

There’s this woman in my building, Elena. Matricola 1265, that’s her ID number. I process her forms sometimes. She’s applied forty-eight times now. Forty-eight. I looked it up once because her number kept coming through. She’s been doing this since she was sixteen. She’s thirty-four now. Forty-eight applications. Forty-seven denials. I see her in the elevator sometimes. She always nods hello. I say hi back. She seems nice enough. Normal, you know? Goes to work, comes home, carries groceries. Just... silent. Always silent.

I don’t really know why she’s classified. It’s not on the forms I see, just her number, her application, the decision. I guess it doesn’t really matter. She just is. There are a lot of them. You see the grey wristbands everywhere once you start noticing. She got approved once actually. Years ago, I saw it in her file when I was processing her latest form. Application number twelve, back in 2035. She got her hour, did fine, got approved for extended speech. Thirty days of being able to talk. Then something happened and it got revoked. Twenty-eight days in, they pulled it back. I don’t know why. The file just says “REVOKED—SEE ATTACHED REVIEW” but I don’t have access to that level. Not my department. The reasons don’t get shared. That’s part of the design. She’s been applying ever since. Thirty-six more times after that. Now forty-eight total. I wonder sometimes what that’s like. Getting it, having it, then losing it again. But I don’t think about it too much. It’s not really my business. I just process the forms.

My life is pretty good, actually. Work is fine. A little boring, but it pays okay. The Mutes are just around. Part of the landscape. Like, there’s one who works on the fourth floor. I see him sometimes. There’s another who gets on the bus at the same stop as me most mornings. You notice the grey wristbands after a while. You just kind of nod at them, they nod back. It’s fine.

I don’t really know what the Review people are looking for when they read the applications. There are probably standards, criteria, something. They don’t publish it. I just move the forms around. Someone way higher up makes the actual decisions. Sometimes I wonder what happens to people like Elena, who keep applying and keep getting denied. Like, does she ever think about just stopping? Accepting it? But then she submits another form and I guess that answers that question. Forty-eight times now. That’s a lot. The thing is, the forms all look the same to me. I can’t tell what makes one person get approved and another get denied. They fill out the same fields, submit the same documentation. Some get through, most don’t. There’s probably a logic to it, but I don’t see it. Not my job to see it, I guess. I’m not mute, so I don’t have to deal with any of this. I can say whatever I want, whenever I want. The system works the way it works. People smarter than me designed it. There are protocols, standards, procedures. It’s all very organized. Very official. I’m just a small part of it.

Elena submitted another form yesterday. Number forty-eight. I processed it. Sent it up to Review. I hope she gets approved this time, I guess. Forty-eight tries is... I mean, that seems like a lot. But I don’t know. Maybe there’s a reason. There’s probably a reason. It’s not really my business. I just move the paperwork. Anyway, that’s basically how it works here. The Archive, the Mutes, the forms, all of it. It’s just normal. Like everything else.

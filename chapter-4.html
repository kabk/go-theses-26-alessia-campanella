Afterword

Context / Notes

This story is based on a real person.

Said was twenty-two years old. He was an immigrant detained at San Vittore prison in Milan. In 2019, he tried to navigate the phone authorization system to make a phone call. He couldn’t complete the requirements. He wrote the phone number down incorrectly: one digit too many. No one in the system noticed. He died by suicide.

In Chapter 2, Castellan doesn’t invent a system. She cites San Vittore’s phone authorization procedures (all real, all documented, all operational since 2011) as evidence that the model is “proven infrastructure.” She references Article 39 of the Italian prison regulations. She notes the occupancy rates. She’s not making an analogy. She’s citing precedent.

San Vittore is a real prison in Milan, it’s been operational since 1879. The phone authorization system Castellan references actually exists. Prisoners need money for a SIM card. They need the phone number of the person they want to call. They need documents proving the recipient’s identity. They need birth certificates. They need phone provider contracts. They need translations if documents are in foreign languages. They need all of this without access to a phone. Since 2011, there have been 127,000 authorization requests. The approval rate is 11.2%.

Castellan justifies Elena’s impossible system by citing a real one. The bureaucratic violence is real; the fiction extrapolates from it. This is the central move, showing how actual bureaucratic violence becomes the template for new violence, justified through reference to old violence. Said died inside the original system. Elena is trapped inside its replica. The horror isn’t just that Elena can’t speak. The horror is that Castellan can point to San Vittore and say, “The model works. It functions in real facilities with real people.” And she’s right. It works exactly as designed.

David Graeber’s The Utopia of Rules argues that bureaucracy isn’t neutral administration but structural violence that hides behind procedures. The violence isn’t in breaking the rules. It’s in following them. That insight shaped everything about Elena’s denials. The system doesn’t need cruel administrators. It needs people like Castellan who apply standards correctly, who follow procedures exactly, who document everything properly. The violence is in the design itself.

Octavia Butler showed me that speculative fiction makes you feel systemic violence in a way theory alone cannot. When you read Elena’s story and feel something, then discover the structure is real, the understanding shifts. You can’t un-feel what you felt.

Kafka understood bureaucratic systems that never explain themselves. You’re trapped and you don’t know why. The system just is, and your confusion becomes proof you don’t belong. The horror isn’t in the mystery of why Elena keeps getting denied. The horror is that there is no mystery. The system is working exactly as designed.

George Orwell showed how systems control reality by controlling language. The most effective control doesn’t announce itself as control. It presents itself as procedure.

The narrator is complicit through indifference, processes forms without examining them closely. Castellan is different, aware of the impossible standards, applying them anyway. The encrypted confession is the only space where honesty exists. Official determination and encrypted note are both true. One is for the record. I made Castellan cite San Vittore not as metaphor but as precedent.

I chose confessional tone because it implicates the writer. I am the narrator. I speak Italian. I speak the language. I can navigate the system. I am not trapped inside it. I have access. I move through institutions more easily. That position lets me see what becomes invisible to people outside: the procedures, the documentation, the impossible requirements that look reasonable on paper. But it also means I can’t know what it feels like to be trapped inside them. I cannot claim Said’s experience or Elena’s. What I am doing isn’t objective analysis—it’s an admission of construction and complicity. Confessional tone becomes both a literary choice and an ethical one: it makes the procedure visible.

Bureaucratic systems cite precedent. They point to existing procedures and say, “This works elsewhere.” That’s how violence scales. One impossible system becomes the model for the next. Castellan cites San Vittore’s 11.2% approval rate as evidence the model functions. She’s right. It does. The system recorded Said’s failure to complete the authorization process. It noted non-compliance. It moved to the next case. The paperwork says they failed to follow procedures. That’s what bureaucratic violence looks like. It doesn’t announce itself. It just functions.

Elena isn’t real. Said was. San Vittore is. The phone authorization system is. The 11.2% approval rate is. The circular documentation requirements are. The monitoring. The recording. The standards designed to ensure failure. I wrote Elena’s story inside the structure that killed Said. I made Castellan cite that structure as proof the model works. Because it does work. Elsewhere. Right now.

This piece cannot restore what was taken. It cannot dismantle the walls of San Vittore or return the voice that silence stole. But perhaps it can hold the system up to light and make visible the machinery that was meant to stay hidden in procedure, in paperwork, in the ordinary weight of bureaucratic routine.

You felt something reading Elena’s story. The trap was there, woven through the forms and denials. Now you know: that trap was real. It caught someone. It was designed to catch someone.

Said existed in that structure. The structure still exists.

That is what remains.
